What Is a Cell?

The cell is the fundamental structural and functional unit of all living organisms. First described by Robert Hooke in 1665 and later elaborated through the Cell Theory by Schleiden, Schwann, and Virchow, cells are the building blocks of life. The human body contains approximately 37.2 trillion cells, each performing highly specialised roles.

Cells are broadly classified into two types: prokaryotic (lacking a membrane-bound nucleus, e.g., bacteria) and eukaryotic (possessing a nucleus enclosed by a membrane, found in animals, plants, and fungi). Human cells are eukaryotic.

Key Organelles and Their Functions

  • Nucleus: Contains genetic material (DNA) and controls cellular activities including gene expression and cell reproduction.
  • Mitochondria: The powerhouse of the cell; generates ATP via oxidative phosphorylation. Also involved in apoptosis (programmed cell death).
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): Rough ER synthesises proteins; smooth ER synthesises lipids and detoxifies chemicals.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Modifies, packages, and distributes proteins and lipids to their destinations.
  • Lysosomes: Digest cellular waste and foreign material using hydrolytic enzymes.
  • Ribosomes: Sites of protein synthesis; translate mRNA into polypeptide chains.
  • Cell Membrane: A phospholipid bilayer that regulates what enters and exits the cell.
  • Cytoskeleton: Provides structural support, enables cell movement, and assists in cell division.

Cell Division

Cells reproduce through two primary mechanisms: mitosis (producing two genetically identical daughter cells for growth and repair) and meiosis (producing four genetically unique cells for sexual reproduction). Errors in cell division can lead to chromosomal abnormalities or cancer.

The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle consists of interphase (G1, S, and G2 phases) and the mitotic phase (M phase). During S phase, DNA is replicated. Checkpoints throughout the cycle ensure genomic integrity before the cell proceeds to division. The tumour suppressor protein p53 plays a critical role in these checkpoints.

Cancer and Uncontrolled Division

When regulatory mechanisms fail, cells can proliferate uncontrollably, forming tumours. Oncogenes promote cell division while tumour suppressor genes inhibit it. Mutations in these genes, whether inherited or acquired, underlie the majority of cancers.

Cellular Communication and Signalling

Cells communicate through chemical signals including hormones, neurotransmitters, and cytokines. Signal transduction pathways convert extracellular signals into intracellular responses, often involving cascades of protein phosphorylation. Key pathways include the MAPK/ERK pathway, the PI3K/AKT pathway, and the JAK-STAT pathway.

Cell Adhesion and the Extracellular Matrix

Cells interact with each other and their surrounding extracellular matrix (ECM) through adhesion molecules such as integrins, cadherins, and selectins. The ECM provides structural support and biochemical cues that regulate cell behaviour including migration, proliferation, and differentiation.

Stem Cells and Differentiation

Stem cells are undifferentiated cells capable of self-renewal and differentiation into specialised cell types. Embryonic stem cells (ESCs) are pluripotent (can become any cell type), while adult stem cells are multipotent (limited to certain lineages). Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs), discovered by Shinya Yamanaka in 2006, are adult cells reprogrammed back to a pluripotent state, revolutionising regenerative medicine and disease modelling.

Differentiation is controlled by transcription factors and epigenetic mechanisms including DNA methylation and histone modification. Environmental signals from surrounding cells also guide differentiation through processes like induction.

Cell Biology and Disease

Dysfunctions at the cellular level underlie virtually all human diseases. Mitochondrial dysfunction is implicated in neurodegenerative conditions such as Parkinson’s and Alzheimer’s disease. Lysosomal storage disorders arise from enzyme deficiencies. Defects in the cytoskeleton are associated with muscular dystrophies. Understanding cellular mechanisms has enabled targeted therapies including monoclonal antibodies, CAR-T cell therapy, and CRISPR gene editing.
