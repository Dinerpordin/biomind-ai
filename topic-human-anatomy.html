The Skeletal System

The human skeleton forms the structural framework of the body, providing support, protection for internal organs, and the mechanical leverage required for movement. In adults, the skeleton comprises 206 bones, although newborns have approximately 270 bones that fuse over time.

Bone Composition and Remodelling

Bone is a dynamic living tissue composed of an organic matrix (primarily type I collagen) and inorganic mineral crystals (mainly hydroxyapatite). Osteoblasts build new bone tissue while osteoclasts break down old bone in a continuous remodelling process. Peak bone mass is typically reached in the late 20s and is influenced by nutrition (calcium and vitamin D), physical activity, and hormonal status.

Joints and Articulations

Joints are classified by structure (fibrous, cartilaginous, synovial) and by the range of movement they permit. Synovial joints, the most mobile type, are lined with articular cartilage and filled with synovial fluid that lubricates the joint surface. Common joint types include hinge joints (elbow, knee), ball-and-socket joints (hip, shoulder), and pivot joints (neck).

The Muscular System

The body contains three types of muscle tissue: skeletal muscle (voluntary, striated, attached to bones), smooth muscle (involuntary, found in organ walls and blood vessels), and cardiac muscle (involuntary, forms the heart wall). The approximately 640 named skeletal muscles work in antagonistic pairs to produce coordinated movement.

Muscle Contraction: The Sliding Filament Theory

Muscle contraction occurs when actin and myosin filaments within sarcomeres slide past one another. An action potential triggers calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum; calcium binds troponin, exposing binding sites on actin. Myosin heads attach, pull, and release in a cyclic power stroke powered by ATP hydrolysis.

The Cardiovascular System

The cardiovascular system consists of the heart, approximately 100,000 km of blood vessels, and 5 litres of blood. The heart pumps about 70 mL per beat and around 5 litres per minute at rest, totalling roughly 2 billion beats over a lifetime.

Circulation Pathways

Pulmonary circulation carries deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle to the lungs for gas exchange, returning oxygenated blood to the left atrium. Systemic circulation distributes oxygenated blood from the left ventricle to the body and returns deoxygenated blood to the right atrium via the venae cavae.

Blood Pressure and Regulation

Blood pressure is regulated by cardiac output, peripheral vascular resistance, and blood volume. The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and the autonomic nervous system play key roles in homeostatic control. Chronic hypertension damages vessel walls and is a major risk factor for stroke and myocardial infarction.

The Nervous System

The nervous system is divided into the central nervous system (CNS) — comprising the brain and spinal cord — and the peripheral nervous system (PNS), which includes all nerves outside the CNS. The PNS is further divided into the somatic (voluntary) and autonomic (involuntary) nervous systems, with the autonomic system subdivided into sympathetic and parasympathetic branches.

The Spinal Cord and Reflexes

The spinal cord carries sensory information to the brain and motor commands from the brain to the muscles. Reflex arcs bypass the brain for rapid responses: a sensory neuron synapses directly onto a motor neuron within the spinal cord, enabling sub-100ms responses that protect the body from injury.

Organ Systems Overview

  • Respiratory System: Exchanges O2 and CO2; the lungs have approximately 480 million alveoli providing ~70 m² of surface area.
  • Digestive System: Breaks down food and absorbs nutrients; the small intestine alone is ~6-7 m long with ~250 m² of absorptive surface.
  • Endocrine System: Glands secrete hormones that regulate metabolism, growth, reproduction, and homeostasis.
  • Urinary System: Kidneys filter ~180 litres of blood plasma per day, producing ~1.5 litres of urine.
  • Immune/Lymphatic System: Defends against pathogens; lymph nodes filter lymphatic fluid and house immune cells.
  • Integumentary System: Skin is the largest organ (~1.7-2.0 m²), regulating temperature, preventing water loss, and sensing the environment.
  • Reproductive System: Produces gametes and sex hormones; supports foetal development in females.
