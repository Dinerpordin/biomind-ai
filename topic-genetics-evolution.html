Foundations of Genetics

Genetics is the scientific study of heredity and genetic variation. Gregor Mendel established foundational principles in the 1860s through his pea plant experiments, demonstrating that traits are inherited in discrete units (now called genes) according to predictable ratios. His laws of segregation and independent assortment remain central to classical genetics.

DNA Structure and the Genetic Code

Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is a double-helix polymer composed of four nucleotide bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), and cytosine (C). The sequence of these bases encodes genetic information. The genetic code is a triplet code: each codon (three consecutive bases) specifies one amino acid or a stop signal. The human genome contains approximately 3 billion base pairs encoding an estimated 20,000–25,000 protein-coding genes.

Gene Expression: Transcription and Translation

Gene expression involves two major steps: transcription (DNA is copied into mRNA by RNA polymerase in the nucleus) and translation (mRNA is decoded by ribosomes to produce a protein). Gene expression is tightly regulated through promoters, enhancers, transcription factors, and epigenetic mechanisms.

Inheritance Patterns

Beyond Mendelian inheritance, genes can display more complex patterns:

  • Autosomal dominant: One copy of a mutant allele causes disease (e.g., Huntington’s disease).
  • Autosomal recessive: Two copies required (e.g., cystic fibrosis, sickle cell anaemia).
  • X-linked recessive: Mutations on the X chromosome affect males more often (e.g., haemophilia, colour blindness).
  • Codominance: Both alleles are expressed simultaneously (e.g., ABO blood groups).
  • Incomplete dominance: Heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype.
  • Polygenic traits: Traits controlled by multiple genes (e.g., height, skin colour, intelligence).

Epigenetics

Epigenetics refers to heritable changes in gene expression that do not involve changes to the DNA sequence itself. Key mechanisms include DNA methylation (typically silencing gene expression), histone modification (acetylation, methylation, phosphorylation), and non-coding RNA regulation. Environmental factors including diet, stress, and toxin exposure can alter the epigenome.

Mechanisms of Evolution

Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace independently proposed natural selection as the primary mechanism of evolution in 1858. Modern evolutionary theory (the Modern Synthesis) integrates Darwinian selection with Mendelian genetics and molecular biology.

The Four Forces of Evolution

  • Natural Selection: Differential reproductive success based on heritable traits that affect fitness in a given environment. Leads to adaptation.
  • Genetic Drift: Random fluctuations in allele frequencies, especially impactful in small populations. Founder effect and bottleneck effect are special cases.
  • Gene Flow (Migration): Transfer of genetic material between populations through migration, reducing genetic differences between populations.
  • Mutation: Random changes in DNA sequence; the ultimate source of all new genetic variation.

Speciation

Speciation is the process by which new species arise. Allopatric speciation occurs when populations become geographically isolated; sympatric speciation occurs without geographic separation, often through polyploidy in plants or ecological divergence. The biological species concept defines a species as a group of organisms capable of interbreeding and producing fertile offspring.

Genomics and Genetic Technology

The Human Genome Project (completed in 2003) sequenced the entire human genome for the first time. Next-generation sequencing (NGS) technologies have dramatically reduced sequencing costs, enabling personalised medicine, population genomics, and ancient DNA analysis.

CRISPR-Cas9

CRISPR-Cas9, adapted from a bacterial immune defence system, enables precise editing of DNA sequences in virtually any organism. It has revolutionised biomedical research and holds promise for treating genetic diseases including sickle cell anaemia, beta-thalassaemia, and certain cancers. Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier were awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2020 for its development.
